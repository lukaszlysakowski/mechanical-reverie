Lineage — after AARON

In 1968 Harold Cohen stopped making paintings and started writing the program that made them. For fifty years AARON drew, and later coloured, on its own. Cohen's rule was total: "I write programs. Programs make drawings." When AARON went somewhere he didn't like, he never fixed the drawing — he changed the program.

Mechanical Reverie is built on that stance. No image here is hand-made; the work is the engine, the genome, the fitness function — the rules that stand in for the eye a machine doesn't have. When the engine couldn't dissolve colour like Jeremy Blake or converge lines like Miguel Ángel Vidal, we didn't retouch a picture; we taught it a new way to see.

Cohen called the signature a system grows on its own — the part never in your plan — its entitality. And he held that an artist designs meaning generators, not meaning communicators: the work isn't a message to decode, it's a thing that starts a thought. This archive doesn't illustrate the tweets it reads; it answers them, in a language slowly becoming its own.

Where AARON reasoned about a world — how faces are built, how plants grow — this engine reasons about the history of that reasoning: the moments computation first met art. It is, in a sense, AARON's grandchild, drawing its own ancestors.
